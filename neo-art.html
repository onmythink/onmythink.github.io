21. ARTS OH THE NEO-CLASSIC ANDROMANTIC PERIODS neoclasical painting​


Answer:

Explanation:

Neoclassical painting was a style of art that emerged in the 18th century and was characterized by a return to classical forms and subjects. Neoclassical painters were inspired by the art and culture of ancient Greece and Rome, and sought to revive the classical ideals of reason, rationality, and harmony.

One of the most famous neoclassical paintings is Jacques-Louis David's "The Oath of the Horatii" (1784). This painting depicts the story of the Horatii, three Roman brothers who pledged to fight for their city against the neighboring Curiatii. The painting features stark, dramatic contrasts of light and shadow, and the figures are depicted in classical poses that emphasize their strength and determination.

Another important neoclassical painter was Jean-Auguste-Dominique Ingres, who was known for his portraits and historical paintings. Ingres' "Napoleon on His Imperial Throne" (1806) is a powerful image of the French emperor, who is depicted in a grandiose pose and surrounded by classical motifs.

The Romantic period, which followed the neoclassical era, was characterized by a renewed interest in emotion, imagination, and individualism. Romantic painters rejected the strict formalism of neoclassical art and instead embraced the power of the imagination and the subjective experience of the individual.

One of the most famous Romantic painters was Eugene Delacroix, whose "Liberty Leading the People" (1830) is an iconic image of the French Revolution. The painting features a powerful image of Liberty leading the people, with a flag in one hand and a musket in the other. The painting is filled with energy and emotion, and the figures are depicted in dynamic poses that convey a sense of movement and action.

Another important Romantic painter was Caspar David Friedrich, who was known for his landscapes that captured the sublime power of nature. Friedrich's "Wanderer Above the Sea of Fog" (1818) is a haunting image of a figure standing atop a mountain, looking out over a misty landscape. The painting captures a sense of awe and wonder at the power of nature, and the figure's solitary pose emphasizes the Romantic ideal of the individual experiencing the sublime.
