9. Which of the following has more potential energy—a 50 kg rock placed at a height of 50 m or a 50 kg rock placed at a height of 70 m? Explain your answer.


Answer:

The rock placed at a height of 70 m has more potential energy than the rock placed at a height of 50 m.

Potential energy is defined as the energy possessed by an object due to its position or configuration. It is proportional to the height or distance the object is from a reference point.

The formula for potential energy is P.E. = mgh, where m is the mass of the object, g is the acceleration due to gravity, and h is the height of the object above a reference point.

In this case, both rocks have the same mass of 50 kg and are subject to the same acceleration due to gravity, g. However, the rock at a height of 70 m has a greater height above the reference point than the rock at a height of 50 m. Therefore, it has a greater potential energy.

Using the formula for potential energy, we can calculate the potential energy of each rock:

The rock at a height of 50 m has a potential energy of P.E. = (50 kg)(9.81 m/s^2)(50 m) = 24,525 J

The rock at a height of 70 m has a potential energy of P.E. = (50 kg)(9.81 m/s^2)(70 m) = 34,335 J

Therefore, the rock placed at a height of 70 m has more potential energy than the rock placed at a height of 50 m.


10. A 4.0-kg mass is placed at (3.0, 4.0) m, and a 7.0-kg mass is placed at (3.0, −4.0) m. What is the moment of inertia of this system of masses about the x axis?


The basic procedure here is to obtain the sum of the indivual moments of inertia in the 2 systems. Let's name the MOI of the 4.0 kg mass as [tex] I_1 [/tex], and the MOI of the 7.0 kg mass as [tex] I_2 [/tex] . Now is this is the case, our goal is to get the total I:

[tex]I = mr^2[/tex]

[tex]I_T = I_1 + 1_2[/tex]

Considering [tex]I = mr^2[/tex], we need to know what r do we use?

Since we're looking for the moment with respect to the x-axis. Imagine if the x-axis was a rod, and the 2 masses are causing it to rotate, right? Knowing this, we only need to take into consideration the distance of the 2 masses from the center of the x-axis. Putting that down mathematically, that's:

[tex]r_1[/tex] = 3
[tex]r_2[/tex] = 3

Now, we plug it back to the equations:

[tex]I_T = I_1 + I_2[/tex]
[tex]I_T = m_1 r_1^2+ m_2 r_2^2[/tex]
[tex]I_T [/tex] = (4.0 kg)(3^2) + (7.0 kg)(3^2) 
[tex]I_T [/tex]= 36 kg/unit + 63 kg/unit 
[tex]I_T [/tex]= 99 kg/unit

∴ The moment of inertia with respect to the x-axis is 99 kg/unit.
