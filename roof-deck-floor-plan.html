1. what are the benefits of careful floor and roof planning​?


Answer:

•Improved traffic flow. With few doors or dividers, an open concept floor plan means that your housemates and guests can easily move around your great room. Open concepts are especially valuable for larger households with busy morning schedules because there’s enough room for everyone to move around as they prepare for the day.

•Increased light. Without partitions or walls between the kitchen, living, and dining space, natural light can easily flow from one room’s window into another room. In an open concept home or working area, more light can also help make the room feel bigger. Learn more about lighting a room with these helpful tips from world-class interior designer Kelly Wearstler.

•Better for communication. Whether talking to your family, entertaining guests, or keeping an eye on children or pets, an open layout makes it easier for sound to travel, provides greater visibility, and increases a sense of togetherness. This floor plan allows you to see what’s happening in your open living areas without being muffled or blocked by closed doors and walls.

•Makes entertaining easier. Open floor plans are great for hosting small parties or game nights because there is ample room for your guests to mill around and socialize. Without the barriers of kitchen doors, you can have a seamless conversation with your guests as you prepare snacks on the kitchen island.

2. How to do a Floor plan with roof framing plan and with front side​


Answer:

Creating a floor plan with roof framing plan and front side can be done using various tools and techniques. Here are the general steps you can follow:

Sketch the outline of the floor plan on paper, including the placement of walls, doors, and windows.Determine the location and spacing of load-bearing walls and columns, as well as the type of roofing system you will use.Draw the roof framing plan, including the placement of rafters, trusses, and any other structural components.Mark on the floor plan the location of any attic spaces or storage areas that will be created by the roof framing.Create a front side elevation view of the building, showing the height and placement of the roof, as well as any architectural features such as dormers or gables.Finally, use a software like AutoCAD, SketchUp, or Revit to create a more detailed and accurate floor plan, roof framing plan, and front elevation view.

It's important to note that creating a floor plan with roof framing plan and front side, specially with a software, might require some technical skills and knowledge in architecture and construction. 12. Identify the parts of a roof plan.​


PARTS OF ROOF PLAN

Answer:

The following are the components of a house's roof:

Roof plane: This is the roof's surface. It's flat, yet it's pitched or angled. It's also known as the roof's field.

Ridge: The top or peak of the roof, where two roof planes meet, is known as the ridge.

Valley: Two pitched roof sides unite and project inward to form a valley. They're always on a lower incline than the roof planes around them.

Dormer: A dormer is a roof feature that extends beyond the roof face. It usually has a window and provides more space and/or light to the interior room.

Abutment: Instead of a roof ridge, an abutment is a location where the roof face meets the home's wall. Abutments are frequently used on modest home additions like entrance canopies, bay windows, attached outbuildings, and garages.

Hip: When two roof faces meet and protrude outward, this is called a hip.

Gable: The triangular wall beneath two roof planes is known as a gable. It's vital to remember that gables aren't found on every roof.

Hipped end: The sloped roof face between two roof hips is known as the hipped end of the roof. The four hipped ends of a pyramid roof. There are only two hipped ends to a hipped roof.

Gable end: The gable end of a roof is the edge of a roof that extends over the gable.

Eave: The border of the outer wall where the roof hangs over.
