5. What do you think are the advantages and disadvantages of land reclamation


ADVANTAGES AND DIS-ADVANTAGES  OF LAND RECLAMATION

Answer:

The advantages and disadvantages of land reclamation

Advantages: More development land has been available. Buildings and infrastructure can be expanded for a variety of reasons.

Disadvantages: In order to get the acreage needed, a lot of flora was eliminated. Corals and marine life can be harmed by land reclamation. When land is being reclaimed, corals are typically relocated. The corals may not be able to survive in that environment and will hence perish. In certain nations, where the operation is large-scale, the corals are not even re-planted; instead, the area on which they live is reclaimed, causing them to die out quickly.

The reclamation of some parts of Manila Bay can have severe implications on the surrounding coastal areas, particularly in Cavite. Cavite is known to be one of the most flood-prone provinces in the country, and the reclamation project can exacerbate the situation. The loss of natural wetlands and mangroves due to the reclamation can also have an adverse impact on the ecosystem, as these areas serve as natural buffers against flooding and storm surges.

It is essential to consider the long-term consequences of the reclamation project, particularly in terms of its impact on the environment and the communities that surround the area. There needs to be a comprehensive plan that takes into account the potential risks and provides adequate measures to address them.

Alternative solutions should also be explored, such as the rehabilitation of existing coastal areas and the promotion of sustainable development practices. 11. Discuss soil sodicity and its reclamation​


Answer:•••••••••••••••••••••••••••••

Soil sodicity ang it's reclamation:

Sodic soil reclamation generally involves the application and incorporation of gypsum into the soil followed by leaching. The rate of reclamation depends on the water intake rate. This rate can become very slow due to inadequate electrolyte concentration in the soil solution to counteract the effects of exchangeable sodium on aggregate stability, clay swelling, and dispersion. Use of successive dilutions of concentrated salt solutions, the high-salt dilution method, has been shown to increase infiltration rates of a sodic soil and to provide sufficient divalent cations for its reclamation. The water requirement for the high-salt dilution method can be reduced to about 1.7 cm of water per cm of soil. The high-salt dilution technique should be considered in irrigated areas with saline drainage waters or in areas close to saline inland lakes and ocean estuaries.••••••••••••••••••••••••••••••••••Explanation:
