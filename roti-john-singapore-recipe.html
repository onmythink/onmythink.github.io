2. John wonderes Will the amount of sugar in the recipe affect the size of a bread loaf ​


Answer:

Explanation:PUTTING THE SCIENTIFIC METHOD TO PRACTICE

Step 1: Problem/ Question

John watches his grandmother bake bread. He asks his grandmother what makes the bread rise. She explains that yeast releases a gas as it feeds on sugar.

John wonders if the amount of sugar used in the recipe will affect the size of the bread loaf?

Step 2: Observation/ Research

John researches the areas of baking and fermentation and tries to come up with a way to test his question. He keeps all of his information on this topic in a journal.

Step 3: Hypothesis

After talking with his teacher and conducting further research, he comes up with a hypothesis.

“If more sugar is added, then the bread will rise higher.”

Step 4: Experiment/ Test

John writes out his procedure for his experiment along with a materials list in his journal. He has both of these checked by his teacher where she checks for any safety concerns.

Independent Variable: John is going to use 50g, 60g, 70g, 80g, and 90g of sugar in his experiment.

Dependent Variable: The size of the loaf of bread

Control Group: Because his grandmother always used 50g of sugar in her recipe, John is going to use that amount in his control group.

Constants: Other ingredients to the bread recipe, oven used, rise time, brand of ingredients, cooking time, type of pan used, air temperature and humidity where the bread was rising, oven temperature, age of the yeast…

Step 5: Collect and Analyze Data

John comes up with a table he can use to record his data.

John gets all his materials together and carries out his experiment

Step 6: Conclusion

John examines his data and notices that 70g of sugar produces the largest loaf. The result at 70 g is better than at 50 g of sugar, his control group. => His hypothesis is accepted.

Step 7: Communicating the Results

John tells his grandmother about his findings and prepares to present his project in Science class.

27. Consider the situation below. Apply the steps of the scientific method in the given situation. John watches his grandmother bake bread. He asks his grandmother what makes the bread rise. She explains that yeast releases a gas as it feeds on sugar. John wonders if the amount of sugar used in the recipe will affect the size of the bread loaf. Question: If you are John, how are you going to solve the problem scientifically? Write your answers below.


Answer:

As John wonders if the amount of sugar used in the recipe will affect the size of the bread loaf, he can use the following steps of the scientific method to solve the problem scientifically:

Formulate a hypothesis: Based on his observation and his grandmother's explanation, John can formulate a hypothesis that increasing the amount of sugar in the recipe will lead to a larger bread loaf.

Design an experiment: John can design an experiment to test his hypothesis. He can prepare two batches of bread using the same recipe, but with different amounts of sugar - one with a higher amount and one with a lower amount. He should ensure that all other ingredients and preparation steps are kept the same for both batches.

Collect data: After baking the bread, John can measure the size of each bread loaf and record the data. He should also note any other observations that may affect the outcome.

Analyze the data: John can analyze the data he collected to determine if there is a correlation between the amount of sugar used in the recipe and the size of the bread loaf. He can use statistical methods to calculate the significance of the correlation, if any.

Draw a conclusion: Based on the results of his experiment, John can draw a conclusion about whether his hypothesis was correct or not. If there was a significant correlation between the amount of sugar and the size of the bread loaf, he can conclude that his hypothesis was correct. If there was no significant correlation, he can conclude that his hypothesis was incorrect.

Communicate the results: Finally, John should communicate his findings to others who may be interested in his experiment. He can share his results in a report, presentation, or other appropriate format.

Answer:

As John, to solve the problem scientifically, I would follow the steps of the scientific method, which includes:

Observation: John observes his grandmother baking bread and asks her what makes the bread rise. He learns that yeast releases gas as it feeds on sugar.

Formulation of a Question: John's question is whether the amount of sugar used in the recipe will affect the size of the bread loaf.

Hypothesis: John formulates a hypothesis based on his observation and his understanding of yeast and its gas-releasing properties. For example, he might hypothesize that increasing the amount of sugar in the recipe will result in a larger bread loaf due to more gas being released by yeast during fermentation.

Design an Experiment: John designs an experiment to test his hypothesis. He would need to keep all other variables constant except for the amount of sugar used in the recipe. For example, he could bake multiple batches of bread, using the same recipe and process, but varying the amount of sugar in each batch (e.g., one with low sugar, one with medium sugar, and one with high sugar).

Data Collection: John would measure and record relevant data during his experiment, such as the size of the bread loaf (e.g., height, width, weight) after baking.

Analysis: John would analyze the data collected from his experiment to determine if there is any correlation between the amount of sugar used in the recipe and the size of the bread loaf. He may use statistical analysis to determine if there are any significant differences.

Conclusion: Based on the results of his analysis, John would draw conclusions about whether his hypothesis was supported or not. For example, if he finds that increasing the amount of sugar resulted in larger bread loaves, he may conclude that there is a correlation between sugar amount and bread size.

Further Experimentation: If John's results are inconclusive or if he wants to further validate his findings, he may design additional experiments with different variations or controls to refine his conclusions.
