25. How did Humanism influence Renaissance art? School of Athens Mona Lisa Statue of David DaVinci’s Sketches


Answer:

One example of how Humanism influenced Renaissance art is the School of Athens, a fresco by Raphael that depicts a group of philosophers and scholars engaged in intellectual pursuits. The figures in the painting are depicted in a naturalistic and lifelike manner, with individualized features and expressions, rather than as stylized or idealized figures as was common in earlier periods.This emphasis on the individuality and humanity of the figures reflects the Humanist belief in the importance of the individual and the value of individual human experience.

Similarly, the Mona Lisa, one of the most famous paintings of the Renaissance, is a portrait that portrays the subject, Lisa del Giocondo, in a naturalistic and lifelike manner. The painting is notable for the subtle and nuanced expression on Lisa's face, which gives the work a sense of individuality and humanity.

The statue of David, by Michelangelo, is another example of how Humanism influenced Renaissance art. The statue depicts the Biblical hero David as a muscular and heroic figure, but with a naturalistic and lifelike depiction of the human form. This emphasis on the individual and the human form reflects the Humanist belief in the inherent value and dignity of human beings.

Finally, Leonardo da Vinci's sketches, which are known for their detailed and accurate depictions of the human body and other natural forms, also reflect the influence of Humanism on Renaissance art. Da Vinci's studies of the human form, which were based on detailed observations of anatomy and movement, demonstrate a focus on the individual and the inherent value of human experience.

Explanation:

Humanism was a philosophical movement that emerged during the Renaissance and was characterized by a focus on the individual and a belief in the inherent value and dignity of human beings. This emphasis on the individual can be seen in the way that Renaissance art depicted people, particularly in portraits and other works that focused on the human form.
