7. Cicle and label dwarf stars,red giants,blue giants and main sequence ​


Answer:

Dwarf stars, red giants, blue giants, and main sequence stars are classifications of stars based on their properties and characteristics, particularly their size, temperature, and age. Here is a brief explanation and labeling of each:

Dwarf stars: These are relatively small stars, typically less than half the size of the sun, with low luminosity and cooler surface temperatures. They are further classified into:

a) Red dwarfs: The smallest and most common type of dwarf star, with a reddish color due to their relatively low surface temperature.

b) White dwarfs: These are the remnants of stars that have exhausted their nuclear fuel and collapsed to a very small size, typically the size of Earth.

Red giants: These are large, relatively cool stars that have exhausted the hydrogen fuel in their cores and have started to burn helium. They are characterized by their reddish color, high luminosity, and large size.

Blue giants: These are massive, hot stars that burn hydrogen at a very high rate, resulting in a bluish color. They are relatively rare and short-lived, typically lasting only a few million years.

Main sequence stars: These are stars that are still in the process of burning hydrogen in their cores and are in a stable state of balance between gravity and radiation pressure. They are characterized by their size, temperature, and luminosity, and are further classified based on their spectral type, from the hottest O-type stars to the coolest M-type stars.

8. Red Giant Star Difination​


Answer:

A red giant star is a type of star that has exhausted the hydrogen fuel in its core and has entered the later stages of its life. It is a large, luminous, and relatively cool star that has expanded to several times its original size.

Red giants are formed from stars that are similar in mass to the sun, and as they age, they begin to run out of fuel. As a result, the core of the star contracts, which causes the outer layers of the star to expand and cool, turning it into a red giant. Red giants can be up to 100 times larger than the sun and can be several hundred times more luminous.

Red giants are also characterized by their reddish color, which is caused by the lower surface temperature of the star. They have a lower surface temperature than the sun, typically ranging from 2,200 to 3,500 Kelvin.

During the later stages of their lives, red giants may also undergo additional changes, such as the fusion of helium into heavier elements, which can cause the star to pulsate or experience mass loss. Eventually, a red giant will exhaust all of its fuel and will either collapse into a white dwarf or explode in a supernova, depending on its mass.

Red giants are important objects in astrophysics because they represent a critical stage in the evolution of stars and provide clues about the future fate of our own sun.

> Because when a star becomes a red supergiant it means that it has left the main sequence or that it has stopped fusing hydrogen at its core and has started fusing heavier elements. Heavier elements give off exponentially higher energies and outward pressure when the undergo fusion, that outward pressure is enough to overcome the gravity of the star and thus the star expands until the outward pressure equalises with the force of gravity that the star itself induces.
