In political science, a multi-party system is a political system in which multiple political parties across the political spectrum run for national elections, and all have the capacity to gain control of government offices, separately or in coalition.[1] Apart from one-party-dominant and two-party systems, multi-party systems tend to be more common in parliamentary systems than presidential systems and far more common in countries that use proportional representation compared to countries that use first-past-the-post elections. Several parties compete for power and all of them have reasonable chance of forming government.

First-past-the-post requires concentrated areas of support for large representation in the legislature, whereas proportional representation better reflects the range of a population's views. Proportional systems may have multi-member districts with more than one representative elected from a given district to the same legislative body, and thus a greater number of viable parties. Duverger's law states that the number of viable political parties is one, plus the number of seats in a district.

Argentina, Armenia, Belgium, Brazil, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Iceland, India, Indonesia, Ireland, the Netherlands, New Zealand, Norway, the Philippines, Poland, Sweden, Tunisia, and Ukraine are examples of nations that have used a multi-party system effectively in their democracies. In these countries, usually no single party has a parliamentary majority by itself. Instead, multiple political parties are compelled to form compromised coalitions for the purpose of developing power blocks and attaining legitimate mandate.

10. multi modals texts are defines as multi modal when it combines two or more semiotic systemsaudio​


Answer:

Overview of multimodal literacy

A multimodal text conveys meaning through a combination of two or more modes, for example, a poster conveys meaning through a combination of written language, still image, and spatial design. Each mode has its own specific task and function (Kress, 2010, p. 28) in the meaning making process, and usually carries only a part of the message in a multimodal text. In a picture book, the print and the image both contribute to the overall telling of the story but do so in different ways.

Images may simply illustrate or expand on the written story, or can be used to tell different aspects of the story, even contradicting the written words (Guijarro and Sanz, 2009, p. 107).

Effective multimodal authors creatively integrate modes in various configurations to coherently convey the meaning required, ‘moving the emphasis backwards and forwards between the various modes' (Cope and Kalantzis, 2009. p. 423) throughout the text.

The complexity of the relationships between the various meaning or semiotic systems in a text increases proportionately with the number of modes involved. For example, a film text is a more complex multimodal text than a poster as it dynamically combines the semiotic systems of moving image, audio, spoken language, written language, space, and gesture (acting) to convey meaning.

What does the term ""multi-party systems"" refer?

A multi-party system is an electoral structure in which various political parties from across the political spectrum compete for public office and both have the potential to control government offices, either jointly or in coalition. Aside from one-party and two-party systems, multi-party systems are more prevalent in parliamentary systems than presidential systems, and much more common in countries that use proportional representation than in countries that use first-past-the-post elections. Several parties vie for power, and both have a good chance of forming a government.

Numerous large and small political parties would have a serious risk of winning office in the overwhelming majority of multi-party structures. Because of the level of rivalry, it is unlikely that one party will dominate the country's legislature, necessitating the formation of a coalition.

Multi-party systems are much more prevalent in countries that use proportional representation as their voting system than in countries that use first past the post. It appears to best represent the complexity of a population's perspectives.

Party Systems

Party systems are categorized into three types: two-party, multiparty, and single-party. This grouping is dependent not only on the number of parties working within a specific country, but also on a number of distinguishing characteristics shared by the three schemes. Two-party and multiparty structures are ways of coordinating political strife within pluralistic societies, and therefore form part of the democratic apparatus. Single parties generally work in conditions where legitimate political disagreement is not accepted. This broad assertion, though, is subject to qualification, since, while single parties do not typically sanction the presentation of points of view that are profoundly contrary to the party line or philosophy, there can be extreme disagreement over policies within the party itself within these limits. Particularly within a two-party or multiparty structure, dialogue may become stymied, and a single alliance of powers may become so entrenched that the political process is compromised.
