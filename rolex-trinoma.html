Oxymoron

A figure of speech known as an oxymoron juxtaposes ideas with contradictory interpretations within a word or phrase to produce an apparent self-contradiction. An oxymoron can be employed as a rhetorical tool to highlight a point or highlight a paradox.

"genuine imitation" is an example of oxymoron.

Oxymorons frequently appear in spoken language. Examples of oxymoron's include "awfully good," "bittersweet," "same difference," and "original copy."In the strict sense, oxymorons are rhetorical devices used consciously by the speaker and meant to be interpreted as such by the audience. The term "oxymoron" has also been used more broadly to refer to accidental or unintentional contradictions, as in the instance of "dead analogies"

A figure of speech is any deliberate departure from a literal statement or accepted use to emphasize, make clear, or accentuate a spoken or written expression. Figures of speech are a critical component of language and can be discovered in spoken literatures, well-crafted poetry and prose, and normal speech.
