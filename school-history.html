1. learning the history of school


Answer:

The history of schools dates back thousands of years to ancient civilizations such as Greece, Rome, and Egypt. In these early societies, education was typically reserved for the wealthy and elite members of society, and was focused on teaching skills such as reading, writing, and mathematics.

During the Middle Ages, education was largely provided by the Church, which established cathedral schools and monastic schools to teach religious texts and prepare young men for the priesthood. Women were generally excluded from formal education during this time.

In the Renaissance period, the emphasis on education shifted from religious instruction to humanistic learning, with a focus on the classics and the study of ancient cultures. This led to the establishment of universities and the widespread availability of education to a wider range of people.

The modern concept of schooling as we know it today began to emerge in the 19th century, with the establishment of public schools and the introduction of compulsory education laws in many countries. The aim was to provide education to all children, regardless of their social status or economic background.

Throughout the 20th century, schools continued to evolve, with a greater emphasis on vocational education and the integration of technology into the classroom. Today, schools continue to play a vital role in society, providing education and training to prepare students for success in the workforce and in life.

20. Explain exhaustively based on your own understanding the "annals school of history"​


Answer:

The Annales school (French pronunciation: [a'nal]) is a group of historians associated with a style of historiography developed by French historians in the 20th century to stress long-term social history. It is named after its scholarly journal Annales d'histoire économique et sociale, which remains the main source of scholarship, along with many books and monographs.[1] The school has been highly influential in setting the agenda for historiography in France and numerous other countries, especially regarding the use of social scientific methods by historians, emphasizing social and economic rather than political or diplomatic themes
