2. An essay an why houses should not be built near rivers?


Answer:

Answer:

Houses should not be built near rivers for various reasons that pose a threat to human life and the environment. Rivers can provide stunning scenery, but building homes close to them can put residents at risk of flooding, erosion, and other natural disasters.

Firstly, flooding is one of the biggest concerns when it comes to building near rivers. During heavy rains or rapid snowmelts, rivers can overflow their banks and cause significant damage to homes, infrastructure, and other property. This can result in severe financial losses and even loss of life, as floodwaters can quickly rise and become extremely dangerous. In addition, many homes near rivers are built on low-lying lands, which are more prone to flooding.

Secondly, rivers can cause soil erosion, which is another major concern for homes near rivers. Over time, the flowing water can erode the banks and weaken the foundation of homes, leading to structural damage. In some cases, homes can even be swept away by the river. This not only puts residents at risk, but also contributes to environmental degradation, as the soil is washed away and contributes to sedimentation in other areas of the river.

Finally, building near rivers can also have negative effects on wildlife and the environment. Many species of fish and other aquatic animals rely on the river for survival and are at risk when homes are built near their habitats. In addition, the construction of homes and other infrastructure near rivers can disrupt the natural flow of water and impact ecosystems.

In conclusion, while rivers can provide a beautiful backdrop for homes, building near them can pose significant risks to human life and the environment. For this reason, it is important to consider alternative locations for building homes that are safe and sustainable.

The first step to flood protection is to determine how likely you are to suffer a flood in your area, and how well your house is built to withstand it. The FEMA Flood Map Service shows general flood risk by region. Enter your address in the search bar to see your area's flood zones, or click "Search All Products" and select your state, county and community to see reports of your region's flood history.

The flood maps include a number of zone markings that indicate the flood risk of a region and its relevant structures, such as bridges, dams and levees. Some of the zones are:

Blue Zones: These are the 1% annual-chance zones. FEMA blue zones are likely to face significant flooding at least once every 100 years. Although 1% may seem like a minimal figure, FEMA still considers this to be a high-risk designation, so you should take adequate steps to flood-proof your home.

Orange Zones: These are 0.2% annual-chance zones. FEMA states that orange zones are likely to face significant flooding at least once every 500 years. Since these regions face lower flooding risk than blue zones, your flood insurance rates will likely be lower. However, you should still make sure your house is not at risk of flooding due to leaks, or excessive surface runoff after rainstorms.

Yellow Zones: These are areas of undetermined flood risk. If you live or are looking to buy property in a yellow zone, we recommend researching the area's flood history. Ask neighbors what kind of flooding they've seen over the course of their time there, and make certain with your appraiser or flood insurance agent that the property is built to withstand any likely flooding.

Blue with Red Stripes: These are regulatory floodways. These zones often include a river and its surrounding floodplain, and are usually kept clear in order to allow water to drain from adjacent flood zones. However, some houses are built in or near regulatory floodways. If this is the case for your home, take extensive precautions to protect the home from flooding.
