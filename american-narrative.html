31. exposition

Exposure writing "explains" or "informs. The majority of our writing is explanatory. When we write exposition, we provide precise information to the reader.

32. argumentation

Argumentative composition is also known as persuasive composition. It appeals to logic, feelings, or ethics (sense of right and wrong).

33. narrative

A narrative is a story, whether it be factual or fictional.

34. description

Description can be used independently, but it is typically found with the other three forms of writing. The purpose of the description is to provide the reader with a "word picture" of a scene, an object, a person, or whatever else is being described.

35. exposition

Exposition

Argumentation

In the context of a debate, argumentation is described as the presentation of opinions supported by facts and/or reasons. When a teenager gives his parents all the logical reasons why he should be permitted to use the car, he is utilizing argumentation.

Narrative

Narratives may be presented as a series of written or spoken words, still or moving images, or any mix of the three. The origin of the word is the Latin verb narrare, which is derived from the adjective gnarus (knowing or skilled).

Description
