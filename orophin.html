8. Why doesn’t each trophic level receive ALL of the energy from the trophic level below it?


The amount of energy available to one trophic level is limited by the amount stored by the level below. Because energy is lost in the transfer from one level to the next, there is successively less total energy as you move up trophic levels.

26. 2. Describe the first trophic level 3. Describe the second trophic level ​


Insight about trophic level,(1st level and 2nd level)

1st level producers

Autotrophs, often known as the producers, are organisms that produce their own sustenance. They form the basis of every food chain. The most prevalent autotrophs are plants and single-celled organisms. Almost all autotrophs use photosynthesis to create "food" (a nutrient called glucose) from sunlight, carbon dioxide, and water. nearly all autotrophs employ a process called photosynthesis. Plants are the most well-known autotroph, although there are several others. Algae are autotrophic, and their bigger forms are known as seaweed. Autotrophs include the phytoplankton, which are microscopic creatures that can live in the water. Autotrophic bacteria are the bacteria that feed on their own waste. Bacteria in active volcanoes, for example, utilize sulfur compounds to make their own food. Chemosynthesis is the name for this process.

2nd level consumers

Organisms that devour the producers make up the second trophic level. Primary consumers, sometimes known as herbivores, are these creatures. Herbivores include deer, turtles, and a variety of birds. Herbivores are eaten by secondary consumers. Secondary consumers are eaten by tertiary consumers. Before a chain reaches its top predator, there may be several layers of customers. Apex predators, often known as top predators, prey on other consumers.

28. what is the stimulant of morphine​


Answer:

Stimulants, sometimes called “uppers,” temporarily increase alertness and energy. The most commonly used street drugs that fall into this category are cocaine and amphetamines.

Prescription stimulants come in tablets or capsules. When abused, they are swallowed, injected in liquid form or crushed and snorted.

SHORT-TERM EFFECTS

The short-term effects of stimulants include exhaustion, apathy and depression—the “down” that follows the “up.” It is this immediate and lasting exhaustion that quickly leads the stimulant user to want the drug again. Soon he is not trying to get “high,” he is only trying to get “well”—to feel any energy at all.

LONG-TERM EFFECTS

Stimulants can be addictive. Repeated high doses of some stimulants over a short period can lead to feelings of hostility or paranoia. Such doses may also result in dangerously high body temperatures and an irregular heartbeat.
