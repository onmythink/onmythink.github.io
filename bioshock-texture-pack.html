21. Types of Soll 1. Clay 2. Loam 3. Sand Characteristics Particles are packed together tightly. It is sticky when wet and has the finest texture. It is a mixture of sand and clay. It contains large amount of decayed plants and animals. It has a fine texture. Particles are coarse and loose​


Answer:

You have correctly identified three types of soil: clay, loam, and sand. Here are some additional characteristics of each type:

1. Clay soil:

- Particles are packed tightly together, which makes the soil heavy and dense.

- When wet, clay soil becomes sticky and malleable.

- It has a fine texture, with particles smaller than 0.002 mm in diameter.

- Clay soil is known for its ability to hold onto nutrients and water, making it good for farming. However, it can also become waterlogged and difficult for plant roots to penetrate.

2. Loam soil:

- Loam is a mixture of sand, silt, and clay in roughly equal proportions.

- It has a crumbly texture that is easy to work with.

- Loam soil is considered ideal for growing plants because it has good drainage and water retention, as well as a balance of nutrients.

3. Sand soil:

- Particles are coarse and loose, making the soil light and well-draining.

- Sand soil contains the largest particles of the three types, with diameters ranging from 0.05 to 2 mm.

- It has a gritty texture and cannot hold onto water or nutrients as well as clay or loam soil.

- Sand soil is often found in areas with low rainfall and can be difficult for plants to grow in without added nutrients and irrigation.

It's important to note that these three types of soil are not the only ones found in the environment, and that many soils have a mixture of these types as well as other components such as organic matter, rocks, and minerals.
