An editorial, leading article (US) or leader (UK), is an article written by the senior editorial people or publisher of a newspaper, magazine, or any other written document, often unsigned. Australian and major United States newspapers, such as The New York Times[1] and The Boston Globe,[2] often classify editorials under the heading "opinion".

Illustrated editorials may appear in the form of editorial cartoons.[3]

Typically, a newspaper's editorial board evaluates which issues are important for their readership to know the newspaper's opinion on.[4]

Editorials are typically published on a dedicated page, called the editorial page, which often features letters to the editor from members of the public; the page opposite this page is called the op-ed page and frequently contains opinion pieces (hence the name think pieces) by writers not directly affiliated with the publication. However, a newspaper may choose to publish an editorial on the front page. In the English-language press, this occurs rarely and only on topics considered especially important; it is more common, however, in some European countries such as Denmark, Spain, Italy, and France.[5]

Many newspapers publish their editorials without the name of the leader writer. Tom Clark, leader-writer for The Guardian, says that it ensures readers discuss the issue at hand rather than the author.[6] On the other hand, an editorial does reflect the position of a newspaper and the head of the newspaper, the editor, is known by name. Whilst the editor will often not write the editorial themselves, they maintain oversight and retain responsibility.[7]

In the field of fashion publishing, the term is often used to refer to photo-editorials – features with often full-page photographs on a particular theme, designer, model or other single topic, with or (as in a photo-essay) without accompanying text.[8]

9. Hello, po how to debate po?Like how to prepare ​


Answer:

Preparing for a debate can be a challenging but rewarding experience. Here are some steps to help you prepare:

Understand the topic: Read and research about the topic you will be debating. Make sure you have a clear understanding of both sides of the argument.

Identify your position: Decide which side of the argument you will be supporting and make a list of arguments and evidence to support your position.

Research your opponent's arguments: It's important to know your opponent's arguments in order to prepare counterarguments.

Practice speaking: Practicing speaking in front of an audience will help you feel more comfortable and confident during the actual debate. You can practice by rehearsing your arguments and speaking in front of a mirror, family or friends.

Know the rules: Make sure you are familiar with the debate format, time limits, and any other rules or guidelines that have been established for the debate.

Stay calm and respectful: Remember to stay calm and respectful during the debate, even if you disagree with your opponent's arguments. Avoid personal attacks or disrespectful language.

Be flexible: Be prepared to adjust your arguments based on what your opponent says or the direction the debate takes.

Anticipate questions: Anticipate questions that your opponent might ask and be prepared with answers and evidence to support your position.

Rebuttal: During the debate, be prepared to respond to your opponent's arguments with a well-reasoned rebuttal that addresses their concerns and counters their arguments.

Summarize your position: At the end of the debate, summarize your main arguments and make a strong closing statement that reinforces your position

Answer:

How to prepare for a debate?

1. Be a Team and Collaborate

Keep in mind that being on a team involves cooperating. Make sure your arguments flow together and follow a logical progression by reading one another's speeches, practicing in front of one another, and reading each other's speeches.

2. Singular Speeches to Write

Break out alone and have each person come up with their ideas before you get together. This will enable your team to gather a wider range of arguments and choose which ones are the most important and ought to be presented as such.

3. Consider the Data

By demonstrating that you have the best-supporting evidence, you will prevail in the discussion. Make sure the evidence you have is accurate and able to be demonstrated by checking it three times. Find a variety of sources to support your claim.

4. Defending Both Positions

Prepare yourself by having counterarguments for the opposing side's position available. By doing this, you can anticipate responses from the opposing team and potentially identify weaknesses in their arguments.

5. Get your speeches ready.

Speech preparation is a crucial step in the process. Have your teammates review your writing when you have finished it on your own. Attempt to have frequent team discussions on your speech so that everyone is aware of potential argument outcomes.

6. Be assured.

You'll feel more confident the better prepared you are. Recognize that every second you spend putting together your strategy takes you one step ahead of the competition. Even if you are feeling uneasy inside during the debate, you may appear confident by constantly referencing the data, pausing when required, and maintaining eye contact.

Good luck with your debate!
