Explanation:

Thevenin's theorem and Norton's theorem are two useful tools for analyzing circuit behavior by reducing complex circuits to simpler, equivalent circuits.

Thevenin's theorem allows us to represent a linear circuit as an ideal voltage source in series with an internal resistance. To solve for the current, voltage, or power in a resistor using Thevenin's theorem, we can follow these steps:

Remove the load resistor from the circuit and replace it with an open circuit.

Determine the open-circuit voltage (V_oc) across the terminals where the load resistor was connected. This is the voltage that the ideal voltage source would produce if it were driving an open circuit.

Determine the internal resistance (R_i) of the circuit by measuring the resistance between the terminals where the load resistor was connected with a multimeter or by calculating it using the appropriate formulas.

To find the current through the load resistor, I_L, we can use Ohm's law: I_L = V_L / R_L

To find the voltage across the load resistor, V_L, we can use the Thevenin voltage equation: V_L = V_oc - I_L * R_i

To find the power dissipated by the load resistor, P_L, we can use the power equation: P_L = V_L * I_L

Norton's theorem allows us to represent a linear circuit as an ideal current source in parallel with an internal resistance. To solve for the current, voltage, or power in a resistor using Norton's theorem, we can follow these steps:

Remove the load resistor from the circuit and replace it with a short circuit.

Determine the short-circuit current (I_sc) through the terminals where the load resistor was connected. This is the current that the ideal current source would produce if it were driving a short circuit.

Determine the internal resistance (R_i) of the circuit by measuring the resistance between the terminals where the load resistor was connected with a multimeter or by calculating it using the appropriate formulas.
