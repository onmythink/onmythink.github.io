10. give us this day our daily bread meaning​


daily bread(Noun) All those things, such as regular prayer, worship and meditation, needed to sustain spiritual life.

Jesus teaches us to pray that God would give us daily bread (Matthew 6:11). Obviously Jesus was not telling His disciples to pray only for bread. But bread was a staple in the diet of the Jews, and had been so for many years. Furthermore, bread was a powerful symbol of God’s provision for His people in the Old Testament. We remember how God cared for the Israelites when they were in the wilderness after their exodus from Egypt. Life in the wilderness was hard, and soon the people began to complain that it would be better to be back in Egypt, where they had wonderful food to eat. In response to these complaints, God promised to “rain bread from heaven” (Ex. 16:4). The next morning, when the dew lifted, there remained behind on the ground “a small round substance, as fine as frost… . It was like white coriander seed, and the taste of it was like wafers made with honey” (vv. 14, 31). When God miraculously fed His people from heaven, he did so by giving them bread.

17. 1. What is the Sacramental meaning of the mullipication of the bread?2. What is the Liturgical meaning of the multiplication of the bread?3. What is the importance of the story the multiplication of the bread to our lives?​


1. The sacramental meaning of the multiplication of the bread, also known as the miracle of the loaves and fishes, refers to the way in which Jesus used the simple act of sharing bread to perform a miracle and feed a large crowd. In the Christian tradition, this event is often seen as a prefiguration of the Eucharist, in which the bread and wine are believed to become the body and blood of Jesus during the Mass.

2. The liturgical meaning of the multiplication of the bread refers to how this event is commemorated and celebrated in Christian liturgy, particularly in the Catholic Church. The miracle of the loaves and fishes is often seen as an example of God's generosity and love for all people, and as a symbol of the Eucharist and the way in which it nourishes and sustains the faithful.

3. The story of the multiplication of the bread is important to our lives because it illustrates the power of faith, the importance of sharing and community, and the transformative nature of God's love. It reminds us that even when we feel like we have very little to offer, God can work through us and use our small offerings to accomplish great things. It also speaks to the idea that when we share what we have with others, there is always enough to go around.
