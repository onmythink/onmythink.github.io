Answer:

1. Propaganda device: Glittering Generalities - the use of positive, general, and vague statements to promote a product or idea without providing specific evidence or facts. The statement "the best & world-class detoxifying and anti-aging juice of all times" is a subjective claim that lacks objective evidence or scientific proof. Therefore, it is not a fact.

2. Propaganda device: Name-calling - the use of negative words or labels to discredit an opponent or group. The statement "Don't be a fool! Alcohol is cool" is an example of name-calling that seeks to manipulate emotions rather than providing facts or logical reasoning. Therefore, it is not a fact.

3. Propaganda device: Plain folks - the use of language, symbols, or values that appeal to ordinary people to promote a product or idea. The statement "Milky Way is the 0 trans fat milk you love" appeals to health-conscious consumers who are looking for healthy and tasty alternatives. This statement is a fact, as it provides objective information about the product's nutritional content.

4. Propaganda device: Bandwagon - the use of social pressure or fear of missing out to encourage people to adopt a particular behavior or belief. The statement "Pres. Duterte joins the people of Pogatas in boodle fight lunch" implies that by joining the President in a popular activity, people can demonstrate their loyalty or patriotism. This statement is a fact, as it describes a real event and the President's participation in it.

5. Propaganda device: Testimonial - the use of endorsements or recommendations from a celebrity or authority figure to promote a product or idea. The statement "Kathryn Bernardo: My everyday use of Olay lotion makes my skin radiant, smooth, white, and glowing. I really love it" suggests that using the product has contributed to the celebrity's success and beauty. This statement is a subjective opinion that lacks objective evidence or scientific proof. Therefore, it is not a fact.

Propaganda is information that is used to try and manipulate or influence people's opinions.

Abstract

This article examines how Toronto’s Filipina/o food establishments have engaged with military practices of eating together across rank through the boodle fight, in which diners eat with their hands, sharing heaps of rice and viands on a central table covered in banana leaves. It asks: what negotiations do urban ethnic restauranteurs make with the Philippines’ history of US imperialism and militarism when they advertise and host boodle fights in their establishments? This study excavates the boodle fight from its West Point origin to its appearances in Philippine militarized civic education and its entry into transpacific diplomatic gatherings and urban diasporic culinary tourism initiatives in North America and the Middle East. It further anchors itself by means of oral histories with restaurateurs and diners—around a boodle fight meal—and explores the entangled narratives of militarism across generations in the Philippine diaspora. While the boodle fight is advertised as a break-down of ranks in favor of a collective Philippine culinary nationalism, it is argued that it never rids itself of military histories and social inequalities reinforced by militarized culture.

A homogeneous mixture is a mixture in which the composition is uniform throughout the mixture.  Often it is easy to confuse a homogeneous mixture with a pure substance because they are both uniform. The difference is that the composition of the substance is always the same.

A heterogeneous mixture is a mixture in which the composition is not uniform throughout the mixture. A heterogeneous mixture consists of two or more phases. When oil and water are combined, they do not mix evenly, but instead form two separate layers. Each of the layers is called a phase.
