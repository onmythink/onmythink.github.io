14. Why can a page have several URLs, but a URL cannot point to several pages?


Answer:

A page can have several URLs because there are often multiple ways to access the same content on a website. For example, a blog post may be accessible through its individual URL, but it may also be accessible through the blog's homepage or through a category page that includes posts on a particular topic. Each of these URLs may lead to the same content, but they may also have slight differences in the query parameters or page structure that distinguish them.

On the other hand, a URL cannot point to several pages because a URL is a unique identifier for a specific web resource. When you enter a URL into your browser, it requests that particular resource from the web server and returns it to you. If a URL could point to multiple pages, it would be difficult to determine which page should be returned when the URL is requested.

It's also worth noting that even if two pages have similar or identical content, it's generally not advisable to use the same URL for both pages. Search engines like Google penalize duplicate content, and it can be confusing for users if they access the same URL and see different content each time

16. What is an example of a 'Description of your copyrighted work' on YouTube requirements for copyright counter-notifications?


A "Description of your copyrighted work" on YouTube refers to a detailed explanation of the original content that you own the copyright to and that was removed from YouTube due to a copyright infringement claim. This description should include information about the nature and scope of your copyrighted work, as well as specific details about how the alleged infringing material on YouTube is related to your work.

Here is an example of a "Description of your copyrighted work" that might be used in a YouTube copyright counter-notification:

"The copyrighted work at issue is a video titled 'My Original Song' that I uploaded to YouTube on January 1, 2020. This video consists of a song that I wrote and recorded, as well as accompanying visual content that I created. The alleged infringing material on YouTube is a video titled 'Song Rip-Off' that was uploaded by User X on February 1, 2020. This video contains a recording of my song, without my permission, along with visual content that is substantially similar to the content in my original video. I am the sole owner of the copyright in my song and the accompanying visual content, and I have not granted permission for User X to use my work in this manner."
