Tumors can be divided into categories based on a variety of features.

Technically, “tumor” just means a swelling, so it could include hematomas and cysts, but generally in modern terminology, it refers to a neoplasia, which is an abnormal growth of cells. The precise meanings get a little snarly, because we use “neoplasia’ to refer to leukemias as well, which are individual cells circulating in the blood and not forming a solid mass, so they are not tumors. Anyway.

They can be divided into benign or malignant. Or cystic and solid. Or primary and metastatic. Etc.

They can also be classified based on the cell type they recapitulate. The most common types of solid tumors are carcinomas, derived from epithelial cells, and then there are other types like sarcomas, derived from mesenchymal cells, lymphomas, derived from lymphoid cells, and melanomas, derived from melanocytes.

Tumors, whether benign or malignant, can often be further classified based on the kind of epithelium they resemble, or their site of origin, so for example there are breast adenomas, breast ductal carcinomas, pancreatic ductal carcinomas, prostate gland carcinomas, etc. Mesenchymal tumors are classified based on the mesenchymal tissue they resemble, such as lipomas (fat tissue), rhabdomyosarcoma (muscle tissue), neuroma (nerve tissue) etc. Lymphomas are classified mainly around the type of lymphoid cell they resemble, so they are divided basically into B-cell and T-cell groups, with an overwhelming array of subtypes.

9. morbidity rate of brain tumor​


Answer:

Almost 36% of Americans with malignant brain or CNS tumors survive for at least five years. Around 31% of people survive after ten years. Following a diagnosis of a malignant brain or CNS tumor, age affects overall survival rates. According to the National Brain Tumor Society, the average five-year relative survival rate for malignant brain tumors is 35.6%. This indicates that 35.6% of those who receive a brain cancer diagnosis five years after the tumor is discovered are still alive.

25. How brain tumor develops?​


Answer:

Primary brain tumors begin when normal cells acquire errors (mutations) in their DNA. These mutations allow cells to grow and divide at increased rates and to continue living when healthy cells would die. The result is a mass of abnormal cells, which forms a tumor.
